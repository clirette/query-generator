Section One: Your Protagonist

What is the full name of your protagonist? If you have multiple protagonists, focus on one for now.

Ex. Junie B. Jones, Jeffrey “The Dude” Lebowski, Harry Potter, Eddard “Ned” Stark

What is the name your character is actually referred to throughout the novel?

Ex. Junie, Jeff/The Dude, Harry, Ned

What is the pronoun you use to describe your character? (Subject form)

Ex. he, she, they

What is your protagonist’s age at the start of the novel?

Ex. 25, 35, 101

Describe your protagonist using a word or short phrase that can go before their name.

Ex. dedicated software engineer, new student, introvert, love-scorned

Before the story begins, what does your character want? What are their goals or dreams? Finish the sentence in the box below.

Ex. to fit in at his new boarding school, to escape their evil step-father’s grasp, a new book to read

Section Two: The Plot

What is the event that upsets your protagonist’s world and sets the story in motion?

Ex. a fellow student is murdered, The Prince invites them to a ball, she discovers a magical spell book

What is the action does your protagonist take as a result of the inciting incident?

Ex. figure out who the murderer is, make The Prince to fall in love with them, protect it at all costs

What is the bad thing that will happen if the protagonist doesn’t take this action? This should have an impact on your protagonist’s hopes and dreams.

Ex. the murderer will strike again, they’ll never escape their evil step-father’s clutches, the book might fall into the hands of an organization bent on ridding the world of magic

Your protagonist is doing A Lot in the first half of the book, but in general terms describe the collection of actions they’re taking to prevent consequences.

Ex. investigates the strange circumstances surrounding the murder; gets ready to attend the ball; searches for the High Librarian, in whose hands the book will be truly safe

As your protagonist dives into the new world of the story, what are they discovering about it and/or themselves? The discovery should complicate their situation.

Ex. another student may be responsible for the crime; they may not need the prince to escape their step-father after all; the conspiracy to rid the world of magic goes deeper than they could ever have imagined.

What is your protagonist’s greatest challenge? The problem that seems unsolvable? This should be a plot point introduced mid-way through the book.

Ex. Jeff discovers he’s the murderer’s new target; their step-father locks them in a tower when they try to leave for the ball; it turns out the High Librarian is working for the evil organization.

Give two conflicting outcomes to the challenge. Best result vs. Worst result. The thing they think they want vs. the thing they actually need. This will act as a hook to make your reader want to learn what happens to your protagonist.

Ex. must solve the murder/end up as the murderer’s next victim; find a way to The Prince/figure out a way to save themselves; learn enough magic to save the book/else be responsible for a world without magic.

Is there an exciting “catchphrase” that you can preface your story with? Something that can instantly pique the interest of your reader? Your book’s version of “When you play the game of thrones, you win or you die…” If you don’t have a great tagline, don’t include one. A cheesy, generic tagline can be worse than no tagline at all.

Ex. In the furthest bookcase, on the tallest shelf, a hidden world of magic awaits...
