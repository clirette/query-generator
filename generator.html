Query Generator

Section One: Your Protagonist

What is the full name of your protagonist? If you have multiple protagonists, focus on one for now.

Ex. Junie B. Jones, Jeffrey “The Dude” Lebowski, Harry Potter, Eddard “Ned” Stark

What is the name your character is actually referred to throughout the novel?

Ex. Junie, Jeff/The Dude, Harry, Ned

What is the pronoun you use to describe your character? (Subject form)

Ex. he, she, they

What is your protagonist’s age at the start of the novel?

Ex. 25, 35, 101

Describe your protagonist using a word or short phrase that can go before their name.

Ex. dedicated software engineer, new student, introvert, love-scorned

Before the story begins, what does your character want? What are their goals or dreams? Finish the sentence in the box below.

Ex. to fit in at his new boarding school, to escape their evil step-father’s grasp, a new book to read

Section Two: The Plot

What is the event that upsets your protagonist’s world and sets the story in motion?

Ex. a fellow student is murdered, The Prince invites them to a ball, she discovers a magical spell book

What is the action does your protagonist take as a result of the inciting incident?

Ex. figure out who the murderer is, make The Prince to fall in love with them, protect it at all costs

What is the bad thing that will happen if the protagonist doesn’t take this action? This should have an impact on your protagonist’s hopes and dreams.

Ex. the murderer will strike again, they’ll never escape their evil step-father’s clutches, the book might fall into the hands of an organization bent on ridding the world of magic

Your protagonist is doing A Lot in the first half of the book, but in general terms describe the collection of actions they’re taking to prevent consequences.

Ex. investigates the strange circumstances surrounding the murder; gets ready to attend the ball; searches for the High Librarian, in whose hands the book will be truly safe

As your protagonist dives into the new world of the story, what are they discovering about it and/or themselves? The discovery should complicate their situation.

Ex. another student may be responsible for the crime; they may not need the prince to escape their step-father after all; the conspiracy to rid the world of magic goes deeper than they could ever have imagined.

What is your protagonist’s greatest challenge? The problem that seems unsolvable? This should be a plot point introduced mid-way through the book.

Ex. Jeff discovers he’s the murderer’s new target; their step-father locks them in a tower when they try to leave for the ball; it turns out the High Librarian is working for the evil organization.

Give two conflicting outcomes to the challenge. Best result vs. Worst result. The thing they think they want vs. the thing they actually need. This will act as a hook to make your reader want to learn what happens to your protagonist.

Ex. must solve the murder/end up as the murderer’s next victim; find a way to The Prince/figure out a way to save themselves; learn enough magic to save the book/else be responsible for a world without magic.

Is there an exciting “catchphrase” that you can preface your story with? Something that can instantly pique the interest of your reader? Your book’s version of “When you play the game of thrones, you win or you die…” If you don’t have a great tagline, don’t include one. A cheesy, generic tagline can be worse than no tagline at all.

Ex. In the furthest bookcase, on the tallest shelf, a hidden world of magic awaits...

Section Three: Meta Data

What is the title of your book? If you don’t have one, use a placeholder. At this stage, your title doesn’t really matter.

Rounding to the nearest 1000th, what is the total word count of your manuscript? (Find out if your word count is appropriate.)

What is the genre of your novel? Clarity is slightly more important than brevity here, so don’t feel as though you have to use a single word.

Ex. mystery, contemporary fantasy, literary, time-traveling romance

Where and when is your story set? Concisely describe the world of your story.

Ex. Warthogs, a fictional Vermont boarding school; the magical kingdom of Cuteprince Land; a magical version of modern-day Edinburgh in a world where librarians are the guardians of ancient magical tomes

What age range is your book aimed at?

Ex. Picture Book, Chapter Book, Middle Grade, YA, Adult

Choose two books you enjoy that are similar to your book, but in different ways, so that when combined, they give the reader more information about the tone and content of your book than your plot summary does. (Read more about comp titles.)

Ex. The Graveyard Book/Kiki’s Delivery Service, Pride & Prejudice/Dracula, Gone Girl/Star Wars.

Will your book have sequels? If so, what will your protagonist do in them?

Ex. solve more mysteries at Murder Academy, explore the world beyond their step-father’s manor, fight against the evil secret society of librarians gone bad

Section Four: You!

Type your real first and last name here. (If you want to give a nickname or shortened version of your first name, that’s fine.)

If you’re absolutely sure you’re going to be using a specific pen name and your real name is entirely off the table, or if you’ve published under a pen name previously, give that name here. Otherwise, leave pen names for a later discussion.

Do you have any professional qualifications related to the writing of this novel? These can be university degrees relevant to the subject of your book, writing awards, writing conferences you’ve attended publications in magazines or journals, etc.

Ex. I have an MA in Medieval Studies from The University of Nottingham. My short story “Medieval Society” was published in The Atlantic last year.

If you don’t have any “official” qualifications, what are some other details about your life that will tell an agent “this is why you should trust me to tell this story.” Try to make these details specific and interesting.

Ex. This novel is loosely based on my experience moving from Montgomery, Alabama to Tokyo. I’ve been reading murder mysteries ever since my mom read Murder on the Orient Express to me as a bedtime story when I was six.

Section Five: The Agent

You’ll be sending your query to multiple agents, but right now, we’ll focus on one. Your dream agent. What is their last name?

What is the honorific you’ll use for this agent? If it is unclear, type their first name.

Ex. Mr., Ms., Dr.

Is there a specific reason you’re submitting to this agent? This is most relevant when you’ve discussed your project with them (at a conference, online, or elsewhere) or if they’ve requested it during a pitching contest. However, if you’re a fan of their clients or listen to their podcast, say so.

ex. I love how funny Client A’s work is, which makes me believe we have similar senses of humor, you mentioned on twitter that you’re looking for a book about medieval society, you favorited my tweet about this project during PitMad

The pledge of common sense

Before I receive my query letter, I, the author of my book, do hereby swear to actually read and edit it before I even think about copy and pasting an auto-generated form letter into an email and sending it on to a real-life agent. Of course I do. Why are you making me read this?

Dear [Honorific] [Agent Name],

[Tagline]...

[Age]-year-old [Defining Characteristic] [Full Name] just wants [Hopes and Dreams], but when [Inciting Incident], [Chosen Name] must [Incited Action] or [Consequence of Untaken Action].

As [Chosen Name] [Preventing Consequences], [Pronoun] discovers [New Discoveries]. When [Greatest Challenge], [Pronoun] has to [Outcome 1] or [Outcome 2].

Complete at [Word Count] words, [Title] is a [category] [genre] set in [setting]. It will appeal to readers of [Comp 1] and [Comp 2]. [Title] has potential for a sequel following [Chosen Name]’s story as [Pronoun] continues to [Sequels].

I am submitting [Title] to you because [Optional Agent Personalization].

[Optional Qualification 1]. [Optional Qualification 2.]. [Optional Personal Detail 1.] [Optional Personal Detail 2.]

Thank you for your consideration.

Yours sincerely, [Author Name] (writing as [Pen Name])

What am I supposed to do with this?

The query letter you’re looking at isn’t perfect, but hopefully it’s a useful starting point. While it hasn’t been done gracefully, it should have helped you identify the information you need for your query, and put it all in roughly the right shape.

Now, it’s your turn to actually turn it into something good. The generator’s goal is to be applicable to as many stories as possible, so now you need to mold the query to your story and give it your voice. Rework the hook into something more exciting. Move the setting information to the first paragraph.. Say “yours cordially” instead of “yours sincerely.” Add a second introductory paragraph to introduce your other narrator, because your story has two protagonist. Make it yours.

This should result in a query on the shorter size of what’s acceptable (250-400 words), so feel free to expand it.

For now, at least all of those elements are in the letter. Whether or not they’re in there well.

But I don’t know what I can change because I don’t know what “the rules” are?

Generally speaking, when editing your query, remember to:

Include the three mandatory sections: About the Book, Metadata, About Me. These are the three main things your query needs to do: Introduce your book in a way that will grab the reader’s attention, tell your reader where your book fits in “The Market,” and let your reader know who wrote the book. Hopefully the Query Generator has helped you with that.

Use business letter formatting. 12 point font. Single spaced. Left alignment. No indentations. A space between paragraphs.

Keep it short. 250-400 words. Remember your goal: to get the agent to pick up the first chapter. Agents can receive hundreds of queries in a week. They don’t have time for wasted words. They won’t wait for you to get to your point. Say what you have to say as quickly as possible

Not include themes. Don’t say this is a story about “friendship and the power or love,” or “children will relate to this story of bullying.” A query letter isn’t a literature class. Don’t analyse your manuscript for your reader. Let the story speak for itself.

Only name a few characters and locations outright. If you’re querying Harry Potter, you’ll want to use Harry’s name in the query, but Aunt Petunia and Uncle Dursley can be “his cruel relatives.” Refer to things by their function in the manuscript and keep your query simple and easy to follow. The more names, locations, and special terms in your query, the more confusing it will be.

And when I’m finished editing?

Congrats! Now here are some extra things to do before you send it off:

Have someone else look over your query before you send it out. Share it with the smartest person you know. An old English teacher. A writing website, like r/writers. Have them judge it on clarity and quality.

Use a site like QueryTracker or Manuscript Wishlist to make a list of agents to send your query out to. Query Tracker will even help you track who your query has gone out to, how long it’s gone out, and how long it typically takes for that agent to respond.

Triple-check you’ve spelled the agents’ names correctly. While some are lenient about a mistake this, others aren’t. Don’t give them a reason to discount your query in the first line.

Triple-check the agents’ submission requirements. Every agency will probably have slightly different requirements, so make sure you’re following the right directions.
